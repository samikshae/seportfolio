Creation of Organic Substrates to Fire LNIS at - Undergraduate Research

2025

Developed a repeatable method for depositing uniform organic layers on metal substrates (Ag, Au, Al) to optimize the performance of Liquid–Neutral Ion Source (LNIS) emitters for mass spectrometry applications. Evaluated how substrate material, coating composition, and deposition conditions affected emission consistency, fragmentation behavior, and overall signal reliability.

Prepared organic–substrate assemblies using amino acids such as glycine, L-Arg HCl, and histidine, systematically testing solution concentrations, deposition techniques, and drying methods. Conducted over 10 LNIS/SIMS electrospray experiments with controlled voltage setups (−3 kV source, +4–5 kV extraction) to characterize emission behavior across substrates. Analysis of TOF-MS data identified target species and contamination signals, supporting comparisons between different substrate types and coating conditions.

Iteratively optimized deposition parameters, including solution molarity, substrate material, and heating temperature, achieving uniform layers that improved measurement reproducibility. The work established a foundation for extending this method to other organic materials and provided critical insight into substrate–coating interactions, ensuring accurate and reliable mass spectrometry results.
